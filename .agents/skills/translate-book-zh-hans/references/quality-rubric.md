# Quality rubric

Use this rubric for drafting, review, and blind comparison. Treat scores as diagnostics, not as permission to average away a hard failure.

## Order of precedence

1. Source integrity and explicit project decisions
2. One-read comprehension in Simplified Chinese
3. Genre-appropriate voice, rhythm, and polish

This corresponds roughly to **信、达、雅**, but the terms are operational here:

- **信**: preserve propositions, relationships, logic, modality, scope, rhetorical posture, and meaning-bearing formatting. It does not require source word order.
- **达**: a target reader can understand and paraphrase the unit once, without reconstructing English syntax.
- **雅**: the prose sounds intentionally written for the target genre and audience. It means apt style, not decorative language.

## Hard fidelity gates

Fail the unit if any of these occur:

- omitted, invented, or contradicted proposition;
- changed actor, relationship, referent, chronology, causality, negation, quantity, modality, or scope;
- unsupported certainty or softened warning;
- term or named-entity error that changes meaning;
- adaptation of an example, person, place, or claim without authorization;
- lost note anchor, omission mark, quotation boundary, or semantically emphasized word;
- target content aligned to the wrong source span.

Do not select a version with a hard failure merely because it is more fluent.

## Scored dimensions after hard gates

Score each dimension from 1 to 5 and cite evidence.

| Dimension | 1 | 3 | 5 |
|---|---|---|---|
| Completeness | material loss/addition | minor recoverable gap | all propositions and constraints preserved |
| Logic | relations distorted | understandable after rereading | causality, contrast, scope, and progression immediately clear |
| Naturalness | translation-like or ungrammatical | usable but stiff | native, unforced Simplified Chinese |
| Voice | wrong register/posture | partly consistent | authorial tone and rhetorical force preserved |
| Rhythm | hard to read aloud | uneven | sentence breath and emphasis feel intentional |
| Terminology | inconsistent/wrong | mostly correct | contextually correct and project-consistent |
| Traceability | source mapping unclear | recoverable | stable unit, note, and decision history intact |

## Confidence labels

- **High**: source meaning, context, and project rule all support the judgment.
- **Medium**: best reading is clear but a lexical or rhetorical alternative remains plausible.
- **Low**: source ambiguity, missing context, or terminology uncertainty could change the choice.

Escalate low-confidence decisions instead of hiding them in polished prose.

## Blind comparison

1. Align versions to the same source content.
2. Remove version names, origins, and prior scores.
3. Apply hard gates independently.
4. Score surviving versions with reasons tied to exact text.
5. Allow a tie when differences are stylistic and evidence does not justify a winner.
6. Reveal identities only after adjudication.
7. If neither version is adequate, synthesize a new candidate and label it as such rather than crediting it to either side.

## Final batch check

Before acceptance, answer all of these:

- Can the Chinese reader state the unit's point after one reading?
- Does the transition from the previous unit make sense?
- Does every source proposition have a target realization?
- Did any natural rewrite alter actor, role, modality, or rhetoric?
- Are terms and names consistent with approved decisions?
- Are emphasis, quotation, omission, and note links preserved?
- Is every nonliteral clarification entailed by the source context?
