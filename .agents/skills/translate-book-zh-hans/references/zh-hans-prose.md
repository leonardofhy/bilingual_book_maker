# Simplified Chinese book-prose reconstruction

Apply these as diagnostics, not mechanical replacements. Preserve the source's rhetorical function even when changing its syntax.

## Rebuild information order

- Prefer a clear subject and main claim before credentials, background, exceptions, or rebuttals.
- Split English sentences that ask one predicate to carry a long list of capabilities or qualifications.
- Merge short English sentences when Chinese needs an explicit relationship such as `毕竟`, `相比之下`, or `更何况`.
- Reorder inside a stable source unit when the Chinese reader needs a different path through the same facts.
- Review adjacent paragraphs as one argument before translating each paragraph separately.

## Remove common English traces

Watch for:

- long preposed modifiers;
- nested `……的，只有……` structures;
- repeated noun subjects where Chinese would use continuity or a direct verb;
- abstract nominalizations instead of people doing things;
- semicolon chains, excessive colons, and mechanical `写道：` transitions;
- resumptive summaries that merely repeat the previous sentence;
- pronouns whose speaker, person, or attachment requires rereading;
- dialogue that omits natural particles such as `的`, `了`, `吧`, or `呢` when the voice requires them.

Do not delete punctuation or add particles by quota. Change them only when the sentence's voice and logic call for it.

## Preserve function when changing form

### Repetition

First identify whether repetition creates insistence, rhythm, a cycle, or escalation.

- Keep it when the repetition itself carries force.
- Convert it into a Chinese progression when identical openers sound mechanical.
- Never collapse repeated claims if the repetition marks separate cases.

### Abstract English noun groups

Turn category labels into a process when Chinese otherwise sounds translated.

- Stiff: `那些盲目乐观者中的幸存者`
- Better pattern: identify who they were, what they experienced, and what they became.

### Passive reactions

Restore the reacting people as subjects when English says an idea “was met by” a response. State who reacted, how, and—only if the source entails it—what they did.

### Quotations and rebuttals

Review the quotation together with the author's response. Reuse plain wording when it exposes the rebuttal. Preserve the quoted speaker's simplicity or overconfidence; do not upgrade it into technical sophistication.

## Controlled explicitation

Add a bridge only when all conditions hold:

1. a target reader otherwise cannot recover the intended link;
2. the link is entailed by the current and adjacent source units;
3. it adds no new fact, example, conclusion, motive, or chronology;
4. it can be identified and defended during the fidelity backcheck.

Prefer a short bridge over a translator's explanatory aside.

## Lexical discipline

- Keep occupational and social roles exact enough for the argument: researcher, scientist, engineer, inventor, neighbor, sibling, and leader are not interchangeable merely because one alternative is more familiar.
- Treat terms contextually. A label used in a simplistic quotation may need different Chinese from the same concept in technical exposition.
- Preserve ambiguity when the source does not establish age, gender, causality, or certainty, unless a documented human decision overrides it.
- Add original spellings for names according to the project policy; do not invent transliterations for one-off bibliographic lists.

## Read-aloud protocol

Read the target without looking at the source and mark every place where the reader:

- runs out of breath;
- loses the subject or speaker;
- must backtrack to attach a modifier;
- hears a word-for-word translation;
- understands every sentence but not the paragraph's point;
- cannot explain why the paragraph follows the previous one.

Revise, then perform the source-fidelity backcheck again. A smoother rewrite is a new translation claim and must be reverified.
